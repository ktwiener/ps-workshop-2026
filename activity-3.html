Activity 3

Inference: Standard Error Estimation

In Activity 3, we estimate standard errors for the IPT-weighted (ATE) and SMR-weighted (ATT) risk differences using three approaches: the robust sandwich variance, the bootstrap, and M-estimation.

Standard error estimation for weighted estimators is non-trivial. The weights are themselves estimated quantities, and their uncertainty should be propagated through to the final estimate. The three methods handle this very differently.

Setup

Run this block first to load packages, data, and reproduce the preprocessing and weight estimation from Activities 1 and 2.

Method 1: Robust sandwich variance

The sandwich (Huber-White) variance estimator is a closed-form, fast approach. It accounts for heteroskedasticity in the weighted regression, but treats the estimated weights as fixed and known — it does not account for the uncertainty from estimating the propensity score.

For the ATE, this means the sandwich SE is generally conservative (too large). For the ATT (which uses inverse-odds weights), it can be either conservative or anti-conservative.

\[\hat{V}_{\text{sandwich}} = (\hat{A})^{-1} \hat{B} \left[(\hat{A})^{-1}\right]^T\]

Method 2: Bootstrap

The bootstrap resamples the entire dataset with replacement and re-estimates everything from scratch on each resample: the propensity score model, the weights, and the outcome model. The standard deviation of the bootstrap estimates is the bootstrap SE.

This correctly captures all sources of variability, including uncertainty from estimating the propensity score. The drawback is computational cost.

Note: 500 bootstrap replicates is sufficient for a workshop illustration. In practice, 1000–2000 replicates are typical. This will take a minute or two in the browser — be patient.

You can also visualize the bootstrap distribution to check that it looks approximately normal, which is a requirement for valid Wald-type confidence intervals.

Method 3: M-estimation

M-estimation propagates uncertainty from all estimation steps — propensity score model and outcome model — simultaneously through a system of stacked estimating equations. It is computationally efficient (no resampling) and asymptotically valid.

An M-estimator \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\) solves:

\[\sum_{i=1}^n \psi\left(\mathbf{Y}_i, \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\right) = \mathbf{0}\]

where \(\psi\) stacks the score equations from the propensity score model with the estimating equations for the weighted means and risk differences.

The variance of \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\) is estimated by the empirical sandwich variance:

\[\hat{V} = \hat{A}^{-1} \hat{B} \left(\hat{A}^{-1}\right)^T, \quad \hat{A} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i \left\{-\psi'\left(Y_i, \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\right)\right\}, \quad \hat{B} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i \psi\left(Y_i, \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\right)\psi\left(Y_i, \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\right)^T\]

The key benefit: because the propensity score equations are part of the stack, their uncertainty is automatically accounted for in the final SEs.

Discussion

  • Why do the estimates match across methods? All three methods estimate the same quantity; they differ only in how they compute uncertainty.

  • Robust sandwich SE: Fast and closed-form, but treats weights as fixed. Generally conservative for the ATE (overestimates SE). For the ATT, it can go either way — see Reifeis & Hudgens (2022) AJE.

  • Bootstrap SE: Resamples the entire data-generating process, capturing all variability including from estimating the propensity score. Computationally expensive but valid.

  • M-estimation SE: Analytically propagates uncertainty from the propensity score model to the outcome, yielding consistent SEs. Efficient (no resampling), but relies on large-sample asymptotics. Can be smaller than the bootstrap when weights are very extreme (like the IPT weights here with a maximum near 500+).

When sample sizes are large and weights are not too extreme, all three methods tend to agree. The ATE weights in this dataset are extreme (very large maximum), so you may see differences — particularly for the bootstrap and M-estimation SEs, which properly account for the extra variability introduced by those extreme weights.
