Activity 2

Propensity Score Estimation and Weighting

In Activity 2, we estimate the propensity score, construct inverse probability of treatment weights for different estimands, assess balance after weighting, and estimate marginal causal effects.

Setup

Each activity page runs independently. Run the block below to reload the data and reproduce the preprocessing from Activity 1 before continuing.

Propensity score model

The propensity score is the probability of receiving t-PA given the observed confounders: \(\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}) = \hat{P}[A=1 \mid \mathbf{L}]\). We estimate it using logistic regression with treatment as the outcome and all confounders as predictors.

Variable selection should be guided by your causal diagram (DAG). Include confounders (causes of both treatment and outcome) and outcome predictors. Exclude mediators (variables on the causal pathway from treatment to outcome).

Propensity score diagnostics

Examine the propensity score distribution within each treatment group. Large regions of non-overlap (“lack of positivity”) indicate covariate profiles where only treated or only untreated people appear — causal effects cannot be estimated there without extrapolation.

What do you notice about the overlap? Are there regions of the propensity score where one treatment group is barely represented?

ATE: Average Treatment Effect

The ATE is the effect of treatment in the entire population — had everyone received t-PA vs. had no one received t-PA. We use inverse probability of treatment (IPT) weights.

\[\hat{\omega}_{i,\text{IPT}} = \frac{I(A_i=1)}{\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}_i)} + \frac{I(A_i=0)}{1-\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}_i)}\]

Stabilized weights scale by the marginal probability of treatment. They have the same mean as 1, produce narrower confidence intervals, and are generally preferred:

\[\hat{\omega}_{i,\text{sIPT}} = \frac{I(A_i=1)\,\hat{P}(A=1)}{\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}_i)} + \frac{I(A_i=0)\,\hat{P}(A=0)}{1-\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}_i)}\]

Balance after IPT weighting

We check covariate balance in the IPT-weighted pseudo-population. The goal is for SMDs to fall below 0.1 for all covariates.

Marginal structural model: ATE

A marginal structural model (MSM) fits a weighted outcome regression in the pseudo-population created by the weights. The weighted regression estimate is equivalent to the marginal (population-average) causal effect.

ATT: Average Treatment Effect in the Treated

The ATT is the effect of treatment among people who were actually treated. We target the treated group using standardized mortality ratio (SMR) weights:

\[\hat{\omega}_{i,\text{SMR}} = I(A_i=1) + \frac{I(A_i=0)\,\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}_i)}{1-\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}_i)}\]

Treated observations keep weight 1. Untreated observations are upweighted by their odds of treatment, making the untreated pseudo-population resemble the treated group.

Why might the ATT be a more relevant estimand than the ATE in this context? t-PA has well-defined eligibility criteria. The ATE averages across everyone — including people for whom t-PA would be contraindicated. The ATT focuses on the people who look like those who actually received treatment: those who cleared the eligibility bar.

ATU: Average Treatment Effect in the Untreated

The ATU asks: what would have been the effect of t-PA among people who were not treated? We target the untreated group using SRR (standardized risk ratio) weights:

\[\hat{\omega}_{i,\text{SRR}} = \frac{I(A_i=1)\left[1-\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}_i)\right]}{\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}_i)} + I(A_i=0)\]

Why might the ATU be less meaningful here? The untreated group includes patients who were ineligible for t-PA (e.g., hemorrhagic stroke, late arrival). Estimating “what would happen if we treated them” requires extrapolating to covariate regions with very few (or no) treated individuals.

Love plot: Balance across weighting methods

A Love plot displays SMDs for all covariates across weighting methods side-by-side. The vertical reference line at 0.1 represents a commonly used threshold for acceptable balance.

Do both weighting approaches achieve balance (SMD < 0.1)? Are there any covariates that remain imbalanced?
