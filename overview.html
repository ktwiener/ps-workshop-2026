Background and Objective

Clinical Background

Tissue plasminogen activator (t-PA) is a thrombolytic agent that breaks down blood clots and has become a standard treatment for acute ischemic stroke. Despite demonstrated benefit in randomized trials, early observational studies from community hospitals found roughly a two-fold increase in in-hospital mortality among patients treated with t-PA — the opposite of what the trials showed.

From three RCTs, the pooled Mantel-Haenszel relative risk estimate for t-PA and death is 1.16 (95% CI: 0.95–1.43) — consistent with no association. The observational data, however, told a different story, raising an important question: what is driving the discrepancy?

Objective

To estimate the effect of t-PA treatment on in-hospital mortality among patients with acute ischemic stroke using observational data, while carefully accounting for confounding.

Why Observational Data Is Challenging

In a randomized trial, the study design ensures that treated and untreated groups are comparable in expectation — both for measured and unmeasured characteristics. In observational settings, treatment is not randomly assigned, and patients who receive t-PA may differ systematically from those who do not in ways that also affect mortality (e.g., stroke severity, age, comorbidities).

This is confounding: the exposure–outcome association is distorted because confounders influence both who receives treatment and who dies.

The Counterfactual Framework

Causal inference asks: what would have happened to each patient had they received (or not received) t-PA? We cannot observe both potential outcomes for the same person — this is the fundamental problem of causal inference.

Let \(A\) denote treatment (1 = t-PA, 0 = no t-PA) and \(Y\) the outcome (1 = in-hospital death). Define \(Y^{a=1}\) as the outcome that would occur if the person received t-PA, and \(Y^{a=0}\) as the outcome under no t-PA. The individual causal effect is \(Y_i^{a=1} - Y_i^{a=0}\), but we observe only one of these.

Instead, we target the average treatment effect (ATE):

\[\text{ATE} = E\left[Y^{a=1} - Y^{a=0}\right]\]

the average effect had everyone in the population received t-PA versus had no one received t-PA.

Identification Conditions

To estimate causal effects from observational data, we need three conditions to hold:

  1. Conditional exchangeability (no unmeasured confounding): \(Y^{a=1}, Y^{a=0} \perp\!\!\!\perp A \mid \mathbf{L}\) Among people with the same covariate values \(\mathbf{L}\), treatment assignment is unrelated to potential outcomes. In other words, we have measured all confounders.

  2. Consistency: \(Y = Y^{a=1} A + Y^{a=0}(1-A)\) The observed outcome under treatment \(A=a\) equals the potential outcome \(Y^{a}\).

  3. Positivity: \(P[A=a \mid \mathbf{L}] > 0\) for all \(l\) where \(f(l) > 0\) Every person, regardless of covariate values, has some non-zero probability of receiving each treatment level. Without this, some covariate patterns are only observed under one treatment, making causal comparison impossible.

Propensity Scores

The propensity score is the probability of receiving treatment conditional on observed covariates:

\[e(\mathbf{L}) = P[A = 1 \mid \mathbf{L}]\]

It was introduced by Rosenbaum and Rubin (1983) as a balancing score: if the full covariate vector \(\mathbf{L}\) is sufficient to achieve conditional exchangeability, so is the scalar propensity score. This is powerful because it collapses a high-dimensional set of confounders into a single number.

In practice, the propensity score is estimated — typically using logistic regression:

\[\text{logit}\left[\hat{e}(\mathbf{L})\right] = \alpha_0 + \boldsymbol{\alpha}^T \mathbf{L}\]

Variable Selection

Variables included in the propensity score model should be:

  • Confounders: causes of both treatment and outcome
  • Outcome predictors: variables associated with the outcome even if not with treatment (improves precision)
  • Excluded: mediators (on the causal pathway from treatment to outcome), pure instruments (causes of treatment only — including them can increase variance without reducing bias)

Variable selection should be guided by a causal diagram (DAG), not by data-driven criteria like stepwise selection.

Diagnostics

After estimating propensity scores, always examine:

  • Distribution: Do treated and untreated groups have similar propensity score ranges? Lack of overlap suggests positivity violations.
  • Balance: After applying weights, do covariate distributions look similar across treatment groups? Standardized mean differences (SMDs) below 0.1 are a common target.

Inverse Probability of Treatment Weighting

One of the primary uses of the propensity score is to construct inverse probability of treatment weights (IPTW) that create a pseudo-population in which confounders are balanced across treatment groups.

The intuition: if a treated person had only a 10% probability of treatment given their covariates, upweighting them by \(1/0.10 = 10\) makes them represent ten similar people — including the nine who (with their same covariate profile) were not treated. After reweighting the full sample this way, the pseudo-population has no measured confounding.

Estimands and Their Weights

Different research questions call for different estimands, each with a corresponding weighting scheme.

Average Treatment Effect (ATE) — the effect had everyone been treated vs. had no one been treated:

\[\hat{\omega}_{i,\text{IPT}} = \frac{I(A_i=1)}{\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}_i)} + \frac{I(A_i=0)}{1-\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}_i)}\]

Average Treatment Effect in the Treated (ATT) — the effect among people who were actually treated:

\[\hat{\omega}_{i,\text{SMR}} = I(A_i=1) + \frac{I(A_i=0)\,\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}_i)}{1-\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}_i)}\]

Treated observations keep weight 1; untreated observations are upweighted by their odds of treatment, making them resemble the treated group.

Average Treatment Effect in the Untreated (ATU) — the effect among people who were not treated:

\[\hat{\omega}_{i,\text{SRR}} = \frac{I(A_i=1)\left[1-\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}_i)\right]}{\hat{e}(\mathbf{L}_i)} + I(A_i=0)\]

Why Does the Estimand Matter Here?

In the t-PA context, consider who actually receives t-PA: patients who arrive at the hospital quickly after symptom onset, have confirmed ischemic stroke, and lack contraindications. This is a clinically selected group. The ATE asks about the effect if everyone with a stroke received t-PA — including patients for whom t-PA is contraindicated. The ATT asks about the effect in people similar to those who actually received it, which may be more clinically actionable.

Standard Error Estimation

Estimating standard errors (SEs) for weighted estimates is non-trivial: the weights are estimated from data, and this estimation step contributes uncertainty that naive model-based SEs ignore.

Three approaches are covered in this workshop:

  1. Robust (sandwich) variance: A fast, closed-form estimator that treats the weights as fixed and known. Conservative for the ATE; may be conservative or anti-conservative for the ATT.

  2. Bootstrap: Resamples the full dataset and re-estimates everything — propensity scores, weights, and outcome model — on each resample. Captures all sources of variability. Computationally expensive.

  3. M-estimation: Stacks estimating equations for the propensity score model and the outcome model, then analytically propagates uncertainty through all steps. Computationally efficient and asymptotically valid.
